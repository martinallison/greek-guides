The first and most obvious thing about Greek for English speakers is that it uses its a different alphabet. This can make it look mysterious and unknowable at first, but you might already recognise some letters.

Some letters might be familiar from maths, various Greek-style yoghurt branding (where they’re totally abused!) or just because they look like some from the Latin alphabet—the alphabet used for English.

The reason some letters look similar is that the Latin alphabet is eventually derived from various historic Greek ones. This might seem weird since some of the ‘same’ letters look so different, or vice-versa, but at one time in history it all made sense.

The alphabet

The Greek alphabet only has 24 letters to learn, and many of them make sounds familiar to English speakers (especially ones that know Spanish or Italian). Here they are in order, with their name, romanisation and basic pronunciation:

Some letters have more than one pronunciation or romanisation. Don’t worry about that for now—we’ll go over detailed pronunciation in the next guide.

See how there are two lowercase letters for σίγμα (sigma): ⟨σ⟩ and ⟨ς⟩? These are the same letter, pronounced the same, but ⟨ς⟩ is used instead of ⟨σ⟩ only if it’s the last letter of a word.

Examples: καφές (kafes—coffee), not καφέσ; σκύλος (skilos—dog) not σκύλοσ.

Letter combinations

You might notice that, so far, there are some sounds common to English that are missing in Greek: bus, door and game, amongst others. These sounds do exist in Greek (more or less), but they’re written with two letters rather than one.

The following table shows those and other letter combinations (although not 100% correct, we’ll call them digraphs from now on) that produce sounds you might not expect:

Digraph
αι ai ferry
αυ af or av sometimes after, sometimes average
γγ ng between sing and golf
γκ gk sometimes golf, sometimes sing
ει ei meet
ευ ef or ev sometimes effect, sometimes Everest
ηυ if or iv sometimes leaf, sometimes beaver
μπ b or mp sometimes bull, sometimes thimble
ντ d or nt sometimes door, sometimes condemn
οι oi meet
ου ou soup
τζ tz pods
υι yi meet

Note: ⟨ηυ⟩ and ⟨υι⟩ are extremely rare.

When reading these digraphs, try to get used to reading them as one thing. For example, with ⟨ει⟩, try seeing the whole thing as ⟨ει⟩, instead of seeing it as ⟨ε + ι⟩. This works for the vast majority of Greek words, but in a later guide, when we introduce word stress, we’ll see it doesn’t always work.

Romanisation

The tables above include the romanisation for each letter and digraph. This simply shows how Greek letters can be transcribed in the Latin alphabet.

Romanisation has little to do with pronunciation in some cases (δέλτα—delta—is romanised as ⟨d⟩, but not pronounced as such) but it’s a pretty good mechanism for getting used to recognising individual Greek letters.

When it comes to reading words, though, it’s better to get used to seeing and reading the Greek letters, and not rely on the romanisations. After the first few guides, we’ll do away with the romanisations when we introduce new Greek words.

Done

That’s pretty much it for the Greek alphabet. After some practice, you’ll be able to make pretty good guesses at how to pronounce many Greek words—but that’s not all there is to pronunciation.

After you start to get the hang of recognising the letters, head to the next guide to learn how to read words and start pronouncing them like a native!
